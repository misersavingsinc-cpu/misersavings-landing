The rate on the landing page is the thing everyone compares. It's also the thing least likely to catch you out. The parts of a savings account worth reading carefully are the ones that aren't in the headline.

Here's what to actually look at.

## The fee schedule

Fees are the most common way a savings account quietly performs worse than advertised.

A monthly maintenance fee is the obvious one, but most high-yield accounts have eliminated these. Look anyway. The less obvious fees are the ones for things you might actually do: excessive withdrawals, wire transfers, paper statements, account closure within 90 or 180 days of opening.

A $5 monthly fee on an account earning 3% APY on a $1,000 balance turns a $30-a-year gain into a $30-a-year loss. The fee doesn't have to be large to matter at small balances.

## Minimum balance requirements

Many accounts advertise their best rate with fine print along the lines of "on balances of $10,000 or more." Below that threshold, the rate drops, sometimes dramatically.

If you're building toward a goal and your starting balance is $400, check what rate you earn at $400, not at $10,000. Some accounts have tiered rates with reasonable entry points. Others are essentially designed for people who already have money and aren't particularly useful for building toward a first cushion.

## Withdrawal limits and transfer timing

Federal regulations in the US used to limit savings account withdrawals to six per month. The rule was suspended in 2020 and hasn't been reinstated, but many banks still impose their own limits, usually framed as a "convenient transfer" limit. Exceeding it may result in a fee, a warning, or in some cases automatic conversion of your account to a checking account.

More practically: how fast can you get the money back to your checking account when you need it? Same-day or next-day transfers are increasingly common but not universal. Some accounts take two to three business days. For an emergency fund, that gap could matter.

> The best savings account is one you can actually access when you need it, not just one that earns well when you don't.

## How interest is calculated

Most accounts compound interest daily and credit it monthly. That's fine and standard.

What's less obvious is that "daily compounding" means your interest earns interest from the day it accrues, not from the day it's actually deposited into your account. The practical difference on a normal savings balance is small, but it's worth knowing what you're looking at when you see the balance fluctuate slightly day to day.

APY (annual percentage yield) is the number that accounts for compounding. APR (annual percentage rate) doesn't. Savings accounts are quoted in APY, which is correct, but if you're comparing any kind of investment product that quotes APR instead, you'll need to adjust to compare fairly.

## FDIC coverage

FDIC insurance protects depositors up to $250,000 per depositor per institution if a bank fails. For most people with a savings account, this coverage is more than adequate.

What it doesn't cover: losses from market investments, cryptocurrency held at an exchange, and anything held at an institution that isn't an FDIC member. Legitimate banks, including online banks, display their FDIC membership. If you can't find it, that's worth investigating before you move money.

The fine print on a savings account is usually short. Reading it before you open one takes about ten minutes and occasionally saves you a frustrating conversation with customer service six months later.
