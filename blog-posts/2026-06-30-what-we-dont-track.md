When you link a bank account to a financial app, you're usually handing over a lot more than you might think. Transaction history going back years. Merchant names. Purchase amounts. Location data attached to card swipes. Income patterns. Sometimes the ability to read your account balance in real time.

Most apps need some of that to function. Some apps collect all of it and more, because data has value and the temptation to collect everything is real.

We thought carefully about where to draw the line. Here's what Miser actually sees, and what it doesn't.

## What we see

When you connect your bank account to Miser, we use it to do one thing: move money. Every Sunday, the total of your week's skips transfers from your checking account to your savings account through our banking partner. That's the transaction.

To do that, we need the ability to initiate an ACH transfer. We don't need your full transaction history. We don't need to see every coffee and Amazon order and gas station stop.

We see your skips, because you log them in the app. We see your goals, because you set them. We see your savings balance in Miser, because we're moving money into it. That's the list.

## What we don't see

We don't pull your transaction history. We don't read your merchant data. We don't know what you bought at Target last Tuesday or how much you spent on delivery this month. We don't build a spending profile.

We also don't sell your data, because we don't have the kind of data worth selling. There's no behavioral profile to monetize. What we have is: you logged a skip, an amount, and a category you chose yourself. That's not a dataset that's useful to advertisers.

> We built Miser around a skip, not a transaction feed, partly because the skip is useful to you. But also because it means we only ever see what you chose to show us.

## Why this matters to us

There's a version of this product that would be more powerful if we had full transaction access. We could suggest which purchases you're most likely to regret. We could identify your spending patterns automatically. We could build the skip log for you instead of asking you to log it yourself.

We decided against it. Not because the technical capability doesn't exist, but because of what it would mean for the relationship.

If Miser is reading your full transaction history, you're not just using a savings tool. You're under observation. Every purchase is potentially data in someone else's system. That changes the feeling of the product, even if nothing bad ever happens with the data.

We'd rather have a product with a slightly smaller surface area that you actually trust, than a more powerful one that makes you wonder what it knows about you.

## The honest trade-off

This approach has a cost. There are things a more data-rich Miser could do that we can't do today. Automatic skip suggestions. Personalized patterns. Category-level analysis.

If user research ever shows us that those features would genuinely help people save more, and that we can implement them in a way people are comfortable with, we'll revisit the decision. We won't pretend this is the only right answer. It's a deliberate choice with trade-offs, and we wanted to be straight about it.
