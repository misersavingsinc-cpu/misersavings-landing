## Two apps, one idea — very different results

You're at the checkout, cart loaded up with something you probably don't need. You close the tab. You feel good. Now what?

That small moment of willpower is the same thing both Miser and Skip-It Savings are built around. But what happens next is where the two apps go in completely different directions.

Skip-It Savings is a simple, well-intentioned app built by indie developer Kana Uchida. You log a skip, you pick a category, and the app adds the skipped amount to a virtual jar. It shows you a running total. There's a wishlist mechanic where you can tie a skip to something you're saving toward. It's clean and the idea is right.

But the money never moves. There's no bank link, no ACH transfer, no actual account. The jar is a number in an app. When you want to "use" what you've saved, you have to manually set that money aside yourself — and at that point, the habit has broken. You've already spent the willpower; now you have to do the paperwork too.

That's not a knock on Kana Uchida. Building any app is hard, and the impulse-skip mechanic is genuinely useful as a mindfulness tool. It just doesn't move money.

## What actually happens when you skip with Miser

When you log a skip in [Miser](https://misersavings.com), you assign it to a goal — paying off a card, building a buffer, saving for something specific. That skip accumulates over the week. On Sunday, Miser pulls the full batch from your linked bank account via ACH and puts it in your vault.

Real money. Real transfer. No manual steps.

The friction is intentional. You're not just counting your good behavior — you're converting it into savings. The gap between "I tracked it" and "it's actually saved" is where most savings habits fall apart, and ACH closes that gap automatically.

There's a fee for this: 3.99% per Sunday batch, capped at $4.99. Withdrawals are free. No subscription. You can stop at any time.

## The virtual counter problem

Virtual counters work well as awareness tools. If you've never tracked your impulse spending, seeing $340 logged over a month is genuinely eye-opening. Skip-It does this well.

But awareness and action are different things. Research on savings behavior consistently shows that people who automate the transfer — even a small one — save significantly more than people who intend to transfer manually. The intention-to-action gap is real, and it's stubborn.

A virtual jar keeps the money exactly where it already was: in your checking account, one click away from a different purchase. The jar number goes up; your savings balance doesn't.

Miser's answer to this is to make the transfer the product. The skip is just the input. The output is money in a separate vault that you have to actively choose to withdraw. That friction, mild as it is, is the point.

## When Skip-It might be the right fit

If you're not ready to link a bank account, or you want to test whether you can actually stick to skipping before committing real money, Skip-It is a reasonable starting point. The wishlist mechanic — tagging a skip to a specific goal item — is a nice motivational touch. It's free to download.

The app has been quietly available for a while, though it hasn't yet accumulated enough ratings to display a score on the App Store. That's the reality of the indie app market — great ideas often live in small audiences.

If you're looking for the skip habit to actually build a savings balance, though, you'll need the money to move.

## The short version

Skip-It Savings helps you notice your impulse spending. Miser helps you keep the money you don't spend. They're solving adjacent problems.

If you've already tried tracking and found yourself manually transferring — or not transferring — this is the upgrade. The habit's the same. The outcome's different.

[Miser is free to join](https://misersavings.com) with no subscription. The fee only applies when money actually moves.
