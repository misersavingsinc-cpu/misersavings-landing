Why "cut your spending by 10%" is the wrong shape of advice

July 21, 2026·2 min read·By Magdalena, co-founder and CEO

"Cut your spending by 10%."

It sounds specific. It has a number in it. It should be actionable.

It isn't. And the reason it isn't is worth understanding, because the same problem shows up in almost every version of the save-more advice you've ever been given.

The problem with percentages

"10% of your spending" is a number that doesn't exist anywhere in your life.

Your life contains a gym membership that costs $29 a month, a delivery habit that ran to $340 last month, a coffee that costs $5 three mornings a week, a car payment, a lease, a grocery run. None of those line items is labeled "10% of total spending." None of them is easy to reduce by exactly 10%.

To cut spending by 10%, you'd have to know your total spending, calculate 10% of it, decide which line items to reduce, and then successfully reduce exactly those line items by exactly the right amounts to hit the target. That's four separate operations, each requiring information and willpower at different times. Most people stall somewhere in the middle and give up.

The advice is technically correct. It's the wrong shape.

Correct advice in the wrong format is worse than no advice at all, because it makes you feel like you tried and failed, rather than like you haven't found the right approach yet.

What the right shape looks like

Good spending advice is:

Concrete enough to act on without research. Not "reduce discretionary spending" but "skip the Friday delivery order this week."

Attached to a specific moment. Not "spend less this month" but "when you open the delivery app tonight, close it and see how you feel in ten minutes."

Small enough to actually do. A behavior you can adopt this week in a single moment is more valuable than a restructuring of your entire budget that requires three Sundays of work to implement.

The 10% instruction fails all three tests. It's abstract, it's not moment-specific, and it requires significant overhead before any actual behavior changes.

The version that works

Instead of "cut spending by 10%," try "find the one spend from last month you regret most, and skip the version of it that comes up next week."

Just one. Not a category overhaul. One specific purchase pattern you already know about, that you already wish you'd handled differently, that will come up again.

That's a 1% instruction, not a 10% one. But it's actionable, and it builds a habit rather than requiring a sustained override of your normal behavior. Once that skip becomes routine, you find the next one. The 10% arrives eventually, in $5 increments, without you ever having to compute it.

The alternative is to keep trying to cut 10% all at once and keep running into the same wall.

The 10% advice works for some people. If you have a clear view of your spending, a relatively predictable income and expense structure, and you enjoy the planning side of personal finance, percentage-based targets are a fine framework. This post is about why the advice fails the majority of people it's aimed at, which is people for whom spending is inconsistent and the monthly review approach has never quite stuck.

The number in advice isn't what makes it good. The shape is what makes it good. Advice you can act on before Friday is worth more than advice that's technically optimal but requires three steps to get started.
