Numbers

The real math on carrying a balance

August 4, 2026·3 min read·By Magdalena, co-founder and CEO

Most people know credit card interest is expensive. The numbers are less familiar than they should be, and the actual cost of a balance you're carrying right now is probably higher than you're picturing.

Here's the math, without softening it.

What a $1,000 balance actually costs

A typical credit card charges somewhere between 20% and 27% APR. Call it 22% for this example. On a $1,000 balance, that's about $220 in interest per year if the balance doesn't move.

But balances move, because the minimum payment moves them, slowly.

A common minimum payment formula is 1% of the balance plus the interest charges, or a flat minimum of around $25 to $35, whichever is higher. On a $1,000 balance at 22% APR, your minimum payment in the first month is roughly $35 to $40.

If you make only minimum payments on that $1,000 balance and add nothing new, it takes roughly five to six years to pay it off, and you'll pay somewhere between $600 and $800 in interest by the time you're done. On a $1,000 purchase. The actual cost of whatever you bought was $1,600 to $1,800.

Balance APR Minimum only Years to clear Total interest
$1,000 22% ~$35/mo ~5.5 yrs ~$700
$3,000 22% ~$75/mo ~7 yrs ~$2,400
$5,000 22% ~$110/mo ~8 yrs ~$4,200

These numbers assume you make the minimum and don't add to the balance. Most people add to the balance.

The interest rate comparison that matters

A high-yield savings account currently pays somewhere in the range of 3% to 5% APY, depending on where rates are. A credit card charges 22%. Those two numbers don't belong in the same financial plan at the same time.

If you're paying 22% interest on a balance and simultaneously putting money into a savings account earning 4%, you are losing 18 percentage points per dollar on the net position. The savings balance feels good, but it's not actually growing relative to the cost of the debt sitting next to it.

Paying down a 22% credit card balance is a guaranteed 22% return. No savings account offers that.

This doesn't mean you should empty your savings to pay off a card. An emergency fund has a different function than an investment, and having no buffer at all creates a new problem. But if you have a meaningful card balance and meaningful savings beyond a basic emergency fund, the math says the savings should probably be smaller and the debt balance should be too.

The version that actually helps

The fastest way out of credit card debt is to pay more than the minimum, consistently, and stop adding to the balance. Neither of those is a sophisticated financial insight. Both of them are harder than they sound.

On the "pay more than minimum" side: even $50 extra per month on a $1,000 balance cuts the payoff time roughly in half and saves several hundred dollars in interest. The additional $50 doesn't have to come from a budget restructuring, it can come from redirecting a few skips.

On the "stop adding to it" side: the balance only compounds if you keep using the card for spending you're not paying off immediately. A card paid in full every month costs $0 in interest. The problem is the rolling balance, not the card itself.

This is general math, not advice for your specific situation. If your debt is large enough to feel unmanageable, a nonprofit credit counseling service (look for NFCC member agencies) can help you understand your options. We're not a financial advisor and this post isn't a substitute for one.

The number most people carry in their head about credit card debt is "it's expensive." The number worth knowing is the actual interest amount on the actual balance, calculated today. It's usually more than the vague sense of "expensive" accounts for.
