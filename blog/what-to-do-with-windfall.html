What to do when a windfall lands

July 28, 2026·3 min read·By Magdalena, co-founder and CEO

A tax refund hits your account. Or a bonus from work. Or a gift that's larger than you expected. Or you sell something and get more than you thought.

A windfall is one of the best financial opportunities most people ever get, and most people quietly waste it. Not through any dramatic mistake, just through the gradual dispersal of money that arrives without a plan.

What usually happens

Money without a destination has a way of becoming money that was spent on things you can't quite remember.

The $1,200 tax refund goes into your checking account. You pay a bill you'd been behind on. You book a trip you'd been putting off. You buy something you'd been waiting to have budget for. Three months later you'd have to think hard to account for the whole amount, and you're not sure the spending added up to something you'd have chosen deliberately.

This isn't a failure of discipline. It's what happens when you don't assign money a job before it dissolves into the general account.

The split decision

The highest-leverage thing you can do with a windfall is decide how to split it before you do anything with it.

Not before you enjoy it, not before you spend any of it, but before the spending begins. A deliberate split, made when the money first arrives, when you're calm and it still feels like a windfall, produces better outcomes than a series of individual spending decisions made over the following weeks when the money has already started to feel normal.

A simple framework: divide it into three before you touch it.

One third goes to something you've been genuinely deferring, a repair, a bill, a thing that needed doing. This is permission to spend on something real.

One third goes directly to savings or debt, immediately, before the money has a chance to mingle with your regular balance. Same day or next day. Set it aside in a way that requires deliberate action to undo.

One third is yours without guilt, for whatever you want. No restrictions. This is the part that makes the other two thirds psychologically sustainable. A windfall with no fun in it doesn't feel like abundance, it feels like an obligation.

The ratios don't have to be exactly equal. The point is making the split explicit rather than letting it happen by default.

The windfall that gets split deliberately on day one almost always produces a better outcome than the one managed transaction by transaction over six weeks.

The debt and savings question

If you have high-interest debt, the split shifts. A credit card balance at 20% or higher is costing you more per year than any savings account is paying you. In that case, the "save" third and the "debt" third should probably both go to the card. The math is unambiguous.

If your debt is lower-rate, a mortgage or a student loan in the 4% to 7% range, the calculation is closer. Paying it down is guaranteed return at that rate. A savings account might earn somewhat less. Neither choice is wrong, and the psychological value of having savings versus the psychological value of reducing debt matters too.

If you have no high-interest debt and a healthy emergency fund, the savings third can go toward a longer-term goal or investment.

This isn't financial advice for your specific situation. The right thing to do with a windfall depends on your debt, your income stability, your goals, and a dozen other factors. If the amount is large enough to matter significantly, a conversation with a fee-only financial advisor is worth the cost.

The main thing is to decide before the money gets comfortable in your checking account. Once it's there and feels normal, it's already mostly gone.
