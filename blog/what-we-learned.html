Built in public

What we learned in our first months

August 11, 2026·3 min read·By Magdalena, co-founder and CEO

When you spend a long time building something, you develop theories about how people will use it. Some of those theories are right. Some of them turn out to be things you believed because you built the product and got attached to your own assumptions.

A few months in, here's what we've actually learned.

The thing we got right

The skip works the way we hoped it would.

We bet that giving people a way to log a purchase they almost made, in the moment they almost made it, would feel different from adding to a savings account separately. It does. People tell us they think about skips differently from saves, the word matters, and that the log of recent skips is something they actually look at.

The skip log is one of the features we argued most about internally. It seemed like a small thing. It turns out to be the part of the product people mention first when they explain it to someone else.

What surprised us

Two things caught us off guard.

The first: how many people use the Goals feature as their primary surface, not the skip log. We built Goals as a supporting feature, something to give context to the skips. For a meaningful percentage of users, the goal is the thing. They open the app to look at how close they are to the flight, the car repair fund, the holiday savings. The skip log is the input; the goal is where the attention goes.

That's a useful signal. It tells us the destination matters at least as much as the behavior.

The second surprise: the Quit Companion feature has almost zero usage. We wrote about why we built it and we believe in it philosophically. But most people who stop using the app just stop. They don't formally quit. They don't go through the exit flow. They close the app and don't reopen it.

That's honest feedback. We're still thinking about what it means.

What we're still figuring out

The Sunday transfer is when everything becomes real. The skip log is in-the-moment, the goal is forward-looking, but Sunday is when actual money moves. Users who engage well with the weekly transfer have better outcomes than users who ignore it or reverse it.

We're not sure yet how to make Sunday feel more like a moment than an automatic process. It's doing its job, the money moves, but we suspect there's a version of it that feels more meaningful without adding friction. We're working on it.

We're also still figuring out how to handle the weeks that go badly. People who have a spending-heavy week sometimes feel like the app is judging them for a low skip count. It isn't, but we haven't fully solved the design question of how to signal that clearly.

Building the product was the easy part. Understanding how real people relate to it is the part that takes longer.

What we're building next

We're not announcing a roadmap here, partly because the roadmap exists but changes, and partly because the most useful things we build tend to come from specific user conversations rather than advance planning.

What we can say: the things we're most interested in right now are around making the Sunday transfer feel like something, making goals more social for people who want that, and improving the experience for users who are saving toward multiple things at once.

If you have something you'd like us to build, or something you wish worked differently, the fastest route is support@misersavings.com. We read all of it.

A note on numbers: this post deliberately doesn't include user counts, retention metrics, or growth figures. We'll share more of that when we have enough data to say something meaningful rather than something that sounds impressive. For now, we're still in the phase of learning what the product actually is for the people using it.

A few months in, the main thing we've learned is that the people who get something from Miser are getting something real. That's enough to keep going.
